# The Look: E-commerce Performance Analysis

## Project Overview
This is the first project in my data analytics portfolio, focusing on **The Look**, a global E-commerce dataset extracted from Google BigQuery. 

Currently, this repository contains the **Initial Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)** of 7 separate datasets. The goal of this first notebook is to understand the structure, distributions, and data quality of the individual tables before performing complex table merges for deeper analysis.

## Data Source & Tools
* **Source:** Google BigQuery Public Datasets
* **Structure:** 7 related tables (`products`, `users`, `events`, `orders_raw`, `order_items`, `inventory_items`, `distribution_centers`)
* **Tools:** Python (Pandas, NumPy), SQL (BigQuery), Jupyter Notebook

## SQL Data Extraction & Pre-processing
To optimize the analysis and ensure data quality right from the source, I wrote custom SQL queries in BigQuery to extract the tables. 

For example, when extracting the `users` table, I noticed the synthetic dataset contained future-dated records (up to the year 2026). I handled this directly in SQL by:
* **Filtering anomalies:** Applied `WHERE created_at <= CURRENT_TIMESTAMP()` to keep only realistic, past data.
* **Protecting Privacy (PII):** Used `EXCEPT(first_name, last_name, user_geom)` to drop Personally Identifiable Information and unused geographical shapes, keeping the dataset clean and compliant.
* **Data Limiting:** Capped the initial extraction at 20,000 rows (`LIMIT 20000`) for this preliminary exploratory phase.

These queries ensure that all downstream analyses start from a clean, privacy-respectful, and time-consistent dataset.

## Preliminary Findings (Key Insights)
By analyzing the descriptive statistics and distributions in Python, I uncovered several interesting insights regarding products and customer demographics:

### Product Insights (`products` table - 20,000 items)
* **Pricing & Margins:** The average product cost is **\\$28.52**, while the average retail price is **\\$59.80**, indicating a healthy overall markup. Prices range from a minimum of \\$0.02 up to \\$999.00.
* **Categories:** I identified the Top 10 most expensive and Bottom 10 cheapest product categories by retail price to understand the store's inventory range.

### User Demographics (`users` table - 20,000 users)
* **Gender & Age:** The customer base is perfectly balanced with a **50/50 Male-to-Female ratio**. The average age of a customer is **~41 years old**, with a remarkably wide age range spanning from **12 to 70**. The core demographic (the middle 50% of users) falls evenly between **26 and 56** years of age.
* **Data Quality (Synthetic Data Collisions):** While there are 20,000 unique `user_id`s, there are only 19,080 unique email addresses. Upon deeper inspection of these duplicates, I found that the same email (e.g., `aarongreen@example.net`) is assigned to users with completely different ages, locations, and creation dates. This proves these are not returning users with multiple accounts, but rather **random collisions generated by the synthetic data creation process**.

### Order Insights (`orders_raw` table - 79,594 orders)
* **Items per order:** Average items per order = **1.45** (min **1**, max **4**). Most orders contain **1–2 items**.
* **Order status distribution:** **Shipped 46%**, **Complete 39%**, **Returned 15%**.
* **Shipping duration (Only 53.89% of shipped orders actually got delivered):** Average delivery time is **~2.5 days** with a maximum close to **5 days**.
* **Shipped but not yet delivered (n = 36,944):** Total items in transit account for **46.09%** of all items, which is a useful metric to quantify the in-transit backlog.

### Order Item Insights (`order_items` table - 20,000 rows)
* **Sales price distribution:** The average sale price is **\\$12.78**, with values ranging from **\\$0.02** to **\\$18.99** (75% of items are priced at or below **\\$15.90**).
* **Very low-priced items (potential discrepancies):** I filtered items with `sale_price < \\$0.50` to check for possible data issues and found a small number of records tied to two accessory products:
  * **Product ID 14235** — cost **\\$0.0083**, retail price **\\$0.02**
  * **Product ID 14159** — cost **\\$0.1774**, retail price **\\$0.49**
  
  These could represent edge cases such as promotional/free items, add-ons, or synthetic pricing artifacts; they are important to flag before calculating KPIs like revenue, discounts, or margins.
* **Item-level status distribution:** The status proportions are consistent with the orders table: **Shipped ~45.5%**, **Complete ~38.8%**, **Returned ~15.6%**.

### Inventory & Profitability Insights (`inventory_items` table - 20,000 rows)
In this dataset, I performed **feature engineering** to calculate key business metrics, including profit margin (`retail_price - cost`), margin percentage, and inventory turnover (`sold_at - created_at`).

* **Inventory Distribution:** The store's inventory is highly concentrated. **Accessories (48%)** and **Activewear (45%)** make up 93% of the stock. Geographically, Distribution Centers 1, 2, and 3 handle the bulk of the inventory (47% combined).
* **Profitability & Margins:** The average profit margin per item is **\\$27.13**, resulting in an excellent average margin percentage of **59%**. Profitability is highly consistent across departments (Women: 60%, Men: 59%).
* **Time to Sell (Inventory Turnover):** On average, it takes **29.4 days** (median 30 days) for an item to be sold after entering the inventory. The maximum time observed is 59 days.
* **Distribution Center Performance (Key Insight):** I compared `days_to_sell` and `margin` across distribution centers. Interestingly, the time to sell is almost identical across all centers (~29 days), indicating consistent operational efficiency. However, the **average margin varies significantly**: Center 8 generates the highest average margin (\\$38.64/item), while Center 9 generates the lowest (\\$16.10/item), likely due to the specific price points of the categories stored in each facility.

### Web Analytics & User Behavior (`events` table - 100k rows sample)
To analyze web traffic effectively, I increased the SQL extraction limit for this table to **100,000 rows**. A smaller sample (e.g., 20,000) returned almost entirely null `user_id`s, which is typical for e-commerce sites where the majority of early funnel events are generated by unauthenticated users.

* **Anonymous vs. Authenticated Traffic:** Out of 100,000 tracked events, **~67.85%** do not have an associated `user_id`. This indicates that more than two-thirds of the website's activity comes from "Guest" or anonymous browsing. There were 91,675 unique sessions, highlighting a high volume of short or single-action visits.
* **Behavior Based on Login Status:** I segmented the events to compare anonymous traffic (`user_id = Null`) vs. logged-in traffic (`user_id = True`). 
  * **The "Cancel" Bug:** **100% of `cancel` events are recorded with a null `user_id`.** In a real-world scenario, a user must be authenticated to cancel an order. This strongly indicates a frontend/backend logging bug where the session drops the user ID when a cancellation is triggered.
  * **Cart Intent:** Conversely, **~58.3%** of `cart` events belong to logged-in users, indicating that users frequently authenticate right before or during the process of adding items to their carts.
* **Traffic Source Consistency:** I analyzed the acquisition channels (Adwords, Email, Facebook, Organic, YouTube) to see if specific platforms drive more logged-in users. Interestingly, the ratio of anonymous to authenticated traffic is highly consistent across **all** traffic sources. No single marketing channel disproportionately drives immediate logins compared to the others.

### Logistics Hubs (`distribution_centers` table - 10 rows)
This is a small geospatial dimension table used to map the fulfillment network. 
* **US-Based Fulfillment for a Global Audience:** All 10 distribution centers are located within the United States. However, as noted in the `users` table analysis, the customer base is highly international (Brazil, Spain, China, etc.). This implies heavy reliance on cross-border shipping, which could explain certain shipping durations and margin pressures.
